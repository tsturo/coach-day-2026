Two sticky notes. No discussion.

Green One thing that genuinely got better with AI in your team.

What the survey said
9

coaches also answered in advance.

Four open questions. Honest answers. Their patterns and yours on the wall tell the same story from two angles — here's what came back from the survey.

Theme 01 · Code review crisis
7/9respondents

The senior became a proofreader.

AI generates huge PRs — 100+ files. Review takes hours. Quality is uncertain. The fun of coding went to the machine; humans got the leftovers.

"I used to build things. Now I check if a machine built them correctly."Paraphrased from survey
Theme 02 · Bottleneck migration
5/9respondents

Coding got fast. Nothing else did.

Requirements gathering, testing, release — untouched. Teams finish code faster but don't ship more. QA is drowning. The bottleneck didn't disappear; it moved.

Theme 03 · Existential motivation crisis
5/9respondents

"What's the point of me?"

Developers losing meaning. Backlogs emptying. Some considering leaving. The range is wide — total AI refusal on one end, thriving on the other. Most are somewhere uncertain in between.

Theme 04 · Pushback against pressure
5/9respondents

"Why are we being forced?"

Unclear why Visma Tech is pushing AI so hard. Is it to be first? Because the CTO said so? To avoid layoffs? Silence breeds worst-case assumptions.

"I'd respect any answer. I just want the real one."Paraphrased from survey
Theme 05 · "AI programmers" conflict
4/9respondents

POs and analysts became developers.

Non-technical roles using AI to make technical decisions — bypassing architecture, security, and the people responsible for them. A new power struggle, poorly acknowledged.

Theme 06 · Cognitive overload
3/9respondents

One agent is already a lot.

Even managing a single AI agent stretches human capacity. Multiple agents overwhelm. Our brains weren't built to track this much parallel output this fast.

C
The team that ran out of work.
  • Throughput tripled. A 6-month backlog is nearly empty at month 2.
  • The partner is delighted but hasn't prepared more requirements.
  • Two people asked you privately: "Will they still need all of us?" You don't have an honest answer.
  • A third stopped using AI entirely — "why would I help make myself redundant faster?"

Case D
D
The silent room.
  • In a retrospective, a developer says out loud what many have been thinking: nobody has explained why the team is being pushed to adopt AI.
  • They ask directly: is this about impressing Visma? About headcount cuts? They say they'd respect any honest answer.
  • The room goes silent. Everyone turns to you — the coach.
  • You can't pass this one up the chain in the moment. You also don't actually know the real "why" yourself.
"I'd respect any answer — I just want the real one."
Missing "why" Leadership gap Coach's uncertainty
Case E
E
The bug that slipped through.
  • Dev throughput doubled. QA capacity didn't.
  • A client found a security vulnerability in AI-generated code that passed review.
  • The reviewer admitted they didn't fully understand that section — but "it looked clean."
  • Team lead wants to slow down. The partner wants to speed up. Trust is shaken on both sides.
"We're not actually going faster. We're finding out about problems later."
Quality vs speed False confidence Trust erosion
Case F
F
The success stories that won't stop.
  • Your team is doing fine. Delivery is steady, quality is holding, people are learning AI tools at a healthy pace.
  • But managers — yours and the partner's — keep hearing polished AI success stories: internal demos, LinkedIn posts, conference talks, "team X shipped in a weekend."
  • Every week you get the same question, in new words: "Why aren't we building like that?"
  • You know most of those stories are survivorship-biased, cherry-picked, or quietly unfinished. But that's a hard argument to win against a highlight reel.
  • Your team is starting to feel the pressure. Some are cutting corners to "look faster." Others are disengaging.
Benchmark pressure Survivorship bias Protecting the team
Before you start

Five minutes of silence.

Read your case alone before anyone talks. The loudest voice in the group shouldn't anchor the diagnosis. Then — two tasks.

Pick a writer One person fills in the worksheet as the group works — not at the end. The worksheet is the artifact; leadership will read it. Make it legible.
Case D — one thing before we start Case D is about leadership not explaining the "why." I am leadership. If your group gets that case, speak freely. I'd rather hear it here than not hear it at all.
Task 01 · 15 min

Peel back the layers.

Use "why?" at least three times. Don't stop at the first answer.

Surface
What's visible? What would a manager see from the outside?
Underneath
What's driving it? What systemic or organizational factors created this?
Core
What's the real fear or need? What is this person actually afraid of losing?
Forcing function If you're writing process problems in the "core" column, you haven't gone deep enough. The core is always about people — identity, safety, meaning, control.
Task 02 · 30 min

Stop. Start. Escalate.

What changes — in behavior, in structure, and in the actual words you'd say on Monday.

Stop
What are we currently doing that makes this problem worse?
Start
What should we begin doing? Action points — practices, rituals, guardrails, experiments. One of these must be the actual conversation you'd have with the person in the case: full sentences, your own words, plus what you would NOT say.
Escalate
What can't we fix at the coach level? Who specifically needs to act — and by when?
Forcing functions The conversation must only make sense for THIS case — if it works for any case, it's too generic. "Escalate" needs a name and a date — not "leadership should communicate better," but "we bring this to [name] by [date]."

04

Each group shares only three things. Task 1 stays on the worksheet — it was work done for the group, not the room.

Presentation format · ~5 min per group

Three things. That's it.

The core — one sentence
What is the person in this case actually afraid of losing?
Start — read the actual words aloud
The opening of the conversation you'd have on Monday.
One Escalate item
With a name and a date.
After each group 1–2 minutes open floor — reactions, challenges, additions. Two groups on the same case present back-to-back so the contrast is visible.
Before we close · 10 min

Back to the green.

We spent an hour on the hard parts. Now walk back to the sticky wall. Read the green notes. What's actually working? What should we keep doing — and spread?

Green The things that got better. Name them out loud. Name who made them better.
Green Pick one to steal. Which green note — not yours — are you taking back to your team?
