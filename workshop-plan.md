# Coach Day: AI — "Expectations vs. Reality"

## Workshop overview

- **Participants:** 20–30 coaches/managers (flexible)
- **Format:** 4 phases + break, ~2h 30min total
- **Group size:** 3–4 people, pre-assigned before the workshop (5–8 groups depending on turnout)
- **Core approach:** Case-based group work — each group receives a realistic scenario built from actual survey responses (9 coaches responded), analyzes it in structured layers, and presents concrete outcomes
- **Language:** Cases, worksheets, and slides in Lithuanian (matches survey source and lets coaches discuss emotionally loaded material in their first language)

---

## Phase 1: Everyone's voice on the wall (15 min)

### Why
Only 9 coaches filled the survey. Without this step, the rest of the room would be spectators in a workshop framed by someone else's answers. Phase 1 comes *before* the survey is ever mentioned — the room speaks first, the survey becomes a second data point after.

### Task
Each person writes two sticky notes (no discussion, just write and stick on the wall):
1. **GREEN** — One thing that genuinely got better with AI in your team
2. **RED** — One thing that got harder, broke, or worries you

### Expected outcome
- A wall of clustered sticky notes — visible, anonymous, owned by everyone
- The green notes ensure positive signals are visible before diving into problems
- Natural clusters will emerge — don't label them yet, just let the room see the patterns

---

## Phase 2: Survey themes + tensions (15 min)

### Why
Sets shared context without attributing anything to individuals. No quotes, no recognizable phrases — just synthesized patterns from the survey and the sticky wall. Interactive, not a monologue — energy from Phase 1 has to carry forward.

### Task
First: present the survey response count (9 coaches) and walk through the 6 themes that emerged, one slide each. Then name the four tensions, one sentence each, no long narration:

1. **Expectation:** AI makes us faster → **Reality:** The bottleneck moved, not disappeared.
2. **Expectation:** Everyone should use AI → **Reality:** Nobody explained why, and pressure feels coercive.
3. **Expectation:** AI democratizes building → **Reality:** Non-developers making technical decisions creates new conflicts.
4. **Expectation:** Developers adapt → **Reality:** The meaning and joy of the work is under threat.

Then: break, and on return, introduce the cases.

### Expected outcome
- Everyone starts from the same baseline understanding
- The "expectations vs. reality" frame gives permission to be honest
- The room sees where pain concentrates — and that they're not alone in it

---

## Break (10 min)

Coffee, bathroom, decompress before the hard work.

---

## Phase 3: Case work in pre-assigned groups (50 min)

### Why
Cases instead of abstract topics force concrete thinking — you can't be vague about a situation with a person in it. Every case carries multiple themes (review overload + motivation + role confusion), so no important tension gets skipped. Pre-assigned groups prevent comfort-zone clustering and ensure hard cases don't get avoided.

### Setup
- Groups of 3–4, pre-mixed by facilitator (mix tenures, teams, temperaments) — 5–8 groups depending on turnout
- All 6 cases are shown to the whole room first; then each group receives ONE assigned case — no choosing
- Each group gets a worksheet with the two tasks below
- Pick one person to fill in the worksheet as the group works — not at the end
- **First 5 min: silent reading.** Everyone reads their case alone before anyone talks. Prevents the loudest voice from anchoring the discussion.

### Case assignment
- Each group gets one case. If there are more groups than cases, double up on A or E (review overload or quality) so two groups can compare approaches in Phase 4.
- **Case D goes to a group with a senior, trusted coach** who will speak plainly in front of leadership.

### Case D acknowledgment (spoken before groups split up)
Facilitator says plainly: "Case D is about leadership not explaining the 'why.' I am leadership. If your group gets that case, speak freely — I'd rather hear it here than not hear it at all. I'll listen before I respond."

---

### The 6 cases

**Case A — The senior who became a proofreader**

A senior developer in your team stopped writing code. They spend most of their day reviewing AI-generated PRs — large ones, 100+ files. Last week they said: "I used to build things. Now I check if a machine built them correctly. I could be anyone." Meanwhile, a mid-level on the same team is thriving — shipping faster than ever, experimenting with agents, pulling others along. The senior is quietly updating their CV.

**Case B — The Product Owner who became a developer**

Your Product Owner spent a weekend building a working prototype with AI tools. On Monday they submitted a PR with 2,000 lines of generated code to the main repo, asking the team to "just review and merge it — it works." The tech lead pushed back on architecture and security concerns. The PO escalated to their manager: "The development team is blocking innovation and slowing us down." Both sides are looking at you.

**Case C — The team that ran out of work**

Your team's throughput tripled with AI. The backlog planned for 6 months is nearly empty at month 2. The partner company is delighted but hasn't prepared more requirements — their BA/planning process can't keep up. Two team members privately asked you: "If we keep going this fast and there's nothing left to do, will they still need all of us?" You don't have an honest answer. A third team member stopped using AI tools entirely — "why would I help make myself redundant faster?"

**Case D — The silent room**

In a retrospective, a developer says out loud what many have been thinking: nobody has explained *why* the team is being pushed to adopt AI. They ask directly — is this about impressing Visma? About headcount cuts? They say they'd respect any honest answer: "I just want the real one." The room goes silent. Everyone turns to you, the coach. You can't pass this up the chain in the moment. You also don't actually know the real "why" yourself.

**Case E — The bug that slipped through**

Dev throughput doubled with AI, but your QA capacity didn't change. A client found a security vulnerability in AI-generated code that passed code review — the reviewer later admitted they didn't fully understand that section but "it looked clean." Trust is shaken — with the client and within the team. The team lead wants to slow down. The partner wants to speed up. A developer says: "We're not actually going faster. We're just finding out about problems later."

**Case F — The success stories that won't stop**

Your team is doing fine. Delivery is steady, quality is holding, people are picking up AI tools at a healthy pace. But managers — yours and the partner's — keep hearing polished AI success stories: internal demos, LinkedIn posts, conference talks, "team X shipped a product in a weekend." Every week you get the same question in new words: "Why aren't we building like that?" You know most of those stories are survivorship-biased, cherry-picked, or quietly unfinished — but that's a hard argument to win against a highlight reel. Your team is feeling the pressure. Some are cutting corners to "look faster." Others are disengaging.

---

### Task 1: Peel back the layers (15 min)

Use "why?" at least 3 times. Don't stop at the first answer.

1. **Surface** — What's visible? What would a manager see from the outside?
2. **Underneath** — What's driving it? What systemic or organizational factors created this?
3. **Core** — What's the real fear or need? What is this person actually afraid of losing?

**Important:** If you're writing process problems in the "core" column, you haven't gone deep enough. The core is always about people — identity, safety, meaning, control.

**Expected outcome:** A layered diagnosis that goes beyond "the process is broken" to "this is what people actually need."

---

### Task 2: Stop / Start / Escalate (30 min)

What changes — in behavior, in structure, and in the actual words you'd say on Monday.

1. **STOP** — What are we currently doing that makes this problem worse?
2. **START** — What should we begin doing? Action points of any kind: practices, rituals, guardrails, experiments, new working agreements, small pilots. **One of these must be the actual conversation** you'd have with the person in the case — full sentences, your own words, plus what you would NOT say. The conversation is an action too; it lives alongside the others.
3. **ESCALATE** — What can't we fix at the coach level? Who specifically needs to act — and by when?

**Flow:**
1. **5 min group** — Draft Stop items and candidate Escalate items together (quick bullets).
2. **10 min group** — Brainstorm Start action points (bullets). Get several on paper.
3. **5 min silent, individual** — Each person writes their own opening lines to the person in the case. Full sentences, not bullets.
4. **8 min group** — Read each conversation version aloud. Pick the strongest, or merge. The writer puts the final conversation (and what NOT to say) on the worksheet alongside the other Start items.
5. **2 min group** — Lock in one Escalate item with a specific name and date.

**Forcing functions:**
- The conversation must only make sense for THIS case — if it works for any case, it's too generic.
- "Escalate" needs a name and a date. Not "leadership should communicate better" — "we bring this to [name] by [date]."

**Expected outcome:** Actionable items with owners, and a realistic, tested script — something a coach would actually say to a real person on Monday.

---

## Phase 4: Presentations + back to green (50 min total)

### Task
One representative per group presents — NOT a full walkthrough. Each group shares only:

1. **The core** (one sentence) — What is the person in this case actually afraid of losing?
2. **Start — read the actual words aloud** — The opening of the conversation you'd have on Monday.
3. **One Escalate item** — With a name and a date.

Task 1 (layers) and the Stop items stay on the worksheet. They were work done *for the group*, not the room. If others want to dig in, they can during discussion.

### Format
- Worksheet visible (wall or screen)
- After each group: 1–2 min open floor — reactions, challenges, additions
- When two groups worked the same case, present back-to-back so the contrast is visible

### Case D handling
If Case D group presents while facilitator is in the room: facilitator listens silently during the presentation. No defending, no clarifying. Respond only after the group finishes and opens the floor. If the group prefers, a co-facilitator can run the debrief for this case.

### Back to the green wall (10 min)
After all groups present, walk the room back to the sticky wall from Phase 1. The workshop has spent an hour on hard cases and problems — this brings the balance back.

- Read the green notes aloud as a group. Name what's actually working.
- Name *who* made them better — credit by name when possible.
- Each person picks one green note that isn't theirs to "steal" — something they're taking back to their own team.

This isn't a feel-good wrap. It's a forcing function: if the hour of case work is all anyone remembers, coaches leave heavier than they arrived. The green wall is evidence that AI adoption isn't only pain — and the "steal one" move turns passive listening into a concrete takeaway.

### Expected outcome
- Shared understanding of how deep these issues go
- A consolidated list of Escalate items with owners and dates (collected from worksheets post-workshop)
- Balance restored by the green wall — coaches leave with at least one concrete idea stolen from someone else
